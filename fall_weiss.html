§ 01 Historical Context

SGS Fall Weiss simulates the opening campaign of the Second World War: Germany's invasion of Poland, launched on 1 September 1939 and concluded with Poland's formal capitulation on 6 October. In five weeks, a nation of 35 million people — possessing a proud and courageous military tradition — was overrun by a two-front assault it had no realistic prospect of resisting alone. The campaign defined the opening character of the war and shaped everything that followed.

The Road to War

The diplomatic context preceding Fall Weiss was a masterwork of manufactured crisis. Hitler originally planned the invasion for 26 August 1939, but British and French diplomatic activity forced a six-day delay. Crucially, some special units conducting operations behind Polish lines did not receive the counter-order in time, leading to scattered clashes before the official start date — a historical detail the game captures in its pre-turn options.

The foundation of German strategic confidence was the Molotov-Ribbentrop Pact, signed on 23 August 1939. This agreement guaranteed Soviet neutrality — and, in a secret protocol, Soviet participation in the partition of Poland. Poland was encircled before a single German unit crossed the border. British and French guarantees to Poland were militarily meaningless without an immediate offensive into Germany; they could not mount one, and did not.

The September Campaign

German forces attacked from three directions: Army Group North from East Prussia and Pomerania in the north; Army Group South from Silesia, Slovakia, and occupied Czechoslovakia in the south. The strategic objective was to encircle and destroy Polish armies west of the Vistula before they could withdraw to defensible river lines. German Panzer and motorised units drove deep into Polish rear areas, disrupting command and preventing the formation of coherent defensive lines. Polish units, many of which fought with exceptional courage and tactical skill, found themselves surrounded before they could concentrate.

The Battle of the Bzura (9–12 September) was the largest Polish offensive action — the Army of Poznań struck the flank of the German march to Warsaw and briefly alarmed German command. Eventually surrounded and destroyed, it nonetheless demonstrated what Polish forces could achieve with operational initiative, and remains a central event in the game's scenario list.

The Soviet Invasion

On 17 September, the Red Army invaded Poland from the east under the Molotov-Ribbentrop protocols, claiming to “protect” Ukrainian and Belarusian minorities. Soviet forces operated with considerably less tactical flexibility than the Wehrmacht — Stalin's purges of 1937–38 had gutted the officer corps — but two simultaneous invasions from opposite directions made further resistance impossible. Warsaw held until 28 September; the last organised resistance collapsed on 6 October.

The “Blitzkrieg” Myth

The term blitzkrieg was coined by Western journalists, not German officers. The Germans called it Bewegungskrieg — war of movement. What distinguished the Wehrmacht was effective combination of superior motorisation, radio communications, combined-arms coordination, and commanders willing to exploit breakthrough. SGS Fall Weiss models these advantages through Panzer movement rates, Kampfgruppe rules, and close air support — without pretending they amounted to a secret formula.

§ 02 Game Overview

SGS Fall Weiss is a turn-based operational wargame covering the full September 1939 Polish campaign. It uses a customised version of the SGS engine incorporating special rules for this campaign — rules that also appear in the forthcoming SGS France 1940 and SGS Balkans titles, forming the “blitzkrieg offensive series.”

How Fall Weiss Differs from Other SGS Titles

  • 2-day turns: Each turn represents two days, giving 19 turns in the main campaign plus a pre-turn. Finer resolution than Overlord's 3-day turns, reflecting the faster operational tempo.
  • Pre-turn variant selection: Before the campaign begins, both players make binding choices about historical versus alternative options. These fundamentally shape the entire game.
  • Three active sides: Axis (Germany + Slovakia), Polish, and Soviet. The Soviet entry at Turn 9 changes the strategic picture for both players simultaneously.
  • Kampfgruppe rules: Combined-arms German formations with special ATK, movement, and breakthrough bonuses representing the integrated tactics of armour, motorised infantry, and artillery.
  • Western Front Pressure: A unique meta-mechanic tracking how long the campaign lasts. Prolonged Polish resistance can trigger a Western Front Allied offensive — automatic German defeat.
Design Philosophy
One stated aim of SGS Fall Weiss is to function as an interactive history book — allowing players to understand how the conflict unfolded rather than executing pre-determined outcomes. The historical variant respects major milestones; the alternative variant opens diplomatic and military possibilities that could plausibly have occurred. Both are legitimate experiences.
Axis Forces (Germany & Slovakia)
Superior in every metric relevant to offensive warfare: motorisation, air power, artillery integration, and command flexibility. Must conquer Poland quickly to avoid Allied intervention in the West. The challenge is not defeating Poland — it is winning decisively and fast enough.
Polish Armed Forces
Brave, capable, and ultimately doomed without Allied relief. Excellent individual unit quality; exceptional national morale. The task is to hold Warsaw and Lwów, bleed the Germans, and last long enough to create political pressure. The hardest side to play well.
Soviet Forces (from Turn 9)
A third party entering mid-campaign. Numerous, armoured, and air-supported, but hampered by post-purge command paralysis. Their arrival forces the Polish player's most agonising strategic choice: eastern deployment versus western concentration.

Scale and Scope

  • Time scale: Each turn = two days. Main campaign = 19 turns (plus pre-turn), September 1 – October 6, 1939.
  • Unit scale: Division, brigade, and regiment level; some battalion-sized units present.
  • Map: Covers all of Poland plus border regions of Germany, East Prussia, Slovakia, and the Soviet Union.

§ 03 Turn Structure & Phase Guide

Each game turn in SGS Fall Weiss represents two days of operations. Understanding phase order is critical because card timing, air windows, and breakthrough chains all depend on which phase is active.

00
Pre-Turn (Campaign Setup)
Both players choose historical or alternative campaign options. Choices are binding for the entire game. Read all options carefully before committing.
01
Cards Phase
Both sides draw and may play strategic cards. Events fire automatically where triggered. Pervitin, Allied pressure, and diplomatic cards may all fire here.
02
Reinforcements Phase
New units arrive at entry points. German timing has variability. Polish replacements improve as popular support events fire.
03
Supply Phase
Supply traced for all units. German Panzer spearheads that outrun supply are immediately vulnerable. Check supply before every advance.
04
Weather Phase
Weather determined. Autumn rain degrades German mobility and reduces Luftwaffe effectiveness. The defender's most reliable natural ally.
05

German air units conduct interdiction or allocate close air support. Polish air reacts where available.
06

Both sides move units. Kampfgruppe formations assembled. German Panzers exploit at high MP rates.

All battles resolve. Combat cards played during resolution. Encircled units fight at reduced effectiveness.
08

Supplied units in range of replacements rebuild. Polish replacements improve as popular support fires. Prioritise key formations.

Unique to Fall Weiss. Checks if Allied pressure has reached the threshold for a Western Front offensive event. Long Polish resistance builds this track.

VP totals checked. Capitulation conditions evaluated. Soviet entry checked from Turn 9 onward. Turn counter advances.
The Pre-Turn — Most Important Decision in the Game
Do not rush the pre-turn options. The historical variant restricts events to those that occurred; the alternative unlocks significant changes: improved Polish air, early mobilisation, possible non-Soviet-invasion, Romanian involvement. Once chosen, pre-turn options cannot be changed.

§ 04 Map & Terrain

Poland in 1939 is predominantly a great plain crossed by major river lines that provided the primary defensive positions available to Polish commanders — and which the Germans worked desperately to outflank before those positions could be occupied in strength.

The River Strategy

The German operational plan was explicitly designed to prevent Poland from occupying the Vistula line in force. As the Polish player, your primary first-four-turn question is: can you get enough forces onto the major river lines before German Panzers cut the roads behind them?

Terrain Strategy Tip — Polish Player
The San River in southeastern Poland and the Narew in the northeast offer the best early defensive positions. Anchor your flanks on these rivers while maintaining a viable Warsaw garrison and you can fight a coherent defence for 6–8 turns. The open plain west of Warsaw is where Polish armies go to die.

Warsaw and Lwów

These cities are not merely VP objectives — they are the capitulation triggers. Poland does not surrender while either holds. As the Axis player, your primary strategic goal is to encircle and reduce both. As the Polish player, every card play and reinforcement that keeps these cities fighting extends the campaign and pressures Germany's western front.

§ 05 Units & Forces

Fall Weiss fields three national forces with sharply different characters. The quality spread is wider than in Overlord — between a German Panzer division and a Polish reserve infantry, it is enormous. Between German and good Polish regular infantry, it is much smaller than popular history suggests.

German Order of Battle
  • Panzer Divisions: The decisive instrument. High ATK, high MOV, breakthrough capable. Must encircle rather than push forward. Irreplaceable if destroyed.
  • Motorised Infantry Divisions: Below Panzer quality but far superior in mobility to standard infantry. Follow Panzer breakthroughs to consolidate gains; cut supply lines behind encircled Polish forces.
  • Standard Infantry Divisions: Solid quality; adequate ATK/DEF. Limited mobility (2 MP). The mass of both Army Groups; grind forward while Panzers race ahead.
  • Mountain Divisions: Carpathian sector specialists. High DEF and reasonable ATK in mountain terrain.
  • Artillery: Support units adding ATK bonus to attacks. Essential for fortified city assaults.
  • Kampfgruppe: Combined-arms formations. See Section 7 for rules.
  • Slovak Army: Smaller, lower quality. Useful for holding freed terrain; not for assault missions.
Polish Order of Battle
  • Infantry Divisions (Regular): Well-trained, high morale. Not significantly inferior to German standard infantry in static defence on good terrain. The backbone of the defence.
  • Reserve Infantry Divisions: Lower quality; slower mobilisation. Arrive later. Useful for garrison and reinforcing river lines.
  • Cavalry Brigades: Mobile, high morale. Excellent in forests, screening, delaying, and supply-line raiding. Not tank-killers — that is a myth — but exceptional raiders and flankers.
  • Armoured / Motorised Units: Limited numbers; older equipment. Useful for counterattacks and plugging gaps.
  • Border Defence Corps (KOP): Eastern frontier troops. Central to the eastern deployment dilemma. See Section 12.
  • Armoured Trains: Move along rail lines; provide fire support; defend junctions. Genuinely useful and historically flavourful.
  • River Flotillas: Small naval units on major rivers. Provide DEF bonus to contested river crossings.
Soviet Order of Battle (enters Turn 9)
  • Rifle Armies: Numerous and large but post-purge command paralysis reduces effectiveness. Good life points; moderate ATK/DEF.
  • Tank Corps: Numerous armour; less operationally flexible than German Panzers. Adequate for the reduced resistance typically faced in eastern Poland.
  • Air Forces: Present; provide CAS to Soviet ground operations.

§ 06 Supply & Logistics

Supply operates on standard SGS principles — trace an unbroken five-region path from unit to source — with a distinctive twist: German Panzer spearheads regularly outrun their supply lines, and this is both historically accurate and genuinely dangerous.

How Supply Works

  • A unit is in supply if it can trace an unbroken path, clear of enemy units or regions, back to a supply source or relay within five regions.
  • Sources: Border entry points for each nation; for Poland, Warsaw and the major eastern cities.
  • Relays: Friendly towns, fixed depots, and logistics units extending supply five regions each.
  • Unsupplied effects: No replacements; movement penalty; combat penalty; extended deprivation causes LP loss.
German Supply Management
Push supply wagons forward before each Panzer surge, not reactively after. A wagon placed in a captured town on Turn 3 extends your supply arc for the Turn 5–6 advance. Panzers at the gates of Warsaw with no supply cannot receive replacements after the assault losses.
Polish Supply Priority
Keep your best units connected to Warsaw's supply network. An encircled Army of Poznań fights at severe penalties. When German columns threaten to close an encirclement, prioritise withdrawal over combat — a unit that escapes can be rebuilt; one eliminated at 0 LP is gone.

§ 07 Combat System

Fall Weiss uses the standard SGS combat system — Battle Morale governs routing, life points govern attrition — with two significant additions: Kampfgruppe combined-arms rules, and encirclement penalties.

Standard Combat Resolution

  1. Attacker and defender commit units to the battle region.
  2. Both sides calculate ATK/DEF totals modified by terrain, leaders, and cards.
  3. Dice rolled against combat factors; life point losses applied to both sides.
  4. Battle Morale compared; a side reaching zero routs and takes Pursuit fire.
  5. Breakthrough-eligible units (armour, motorised, leaders) may advance after victory.

Kampfgruppe Rules

A Kampfgruppe — Panzer division + motorised division + artillery — receives bonuses to ATK, movement (at the fastest component's rate), and breakthrough range. Never send a Panzer division forward alone when you can form a proper Kampfgruppe.

Forming Effective Kampfgruppen
The ideal combination: one Panzer division (ATK bonus + breakthrough), one motorised division (consolidates gains), one artillery unit (suppresses defenders, reduces fortress bonuses). This combination — historically the basis of every German armoured thrust in 1939 — consistently outperforms the sum of its parts.

Encirclement

When all adjacent regions are occupied by enemy forces, the encircled unit suffers combat and morale penalties. Encircled units cannot receive reinforcements. Extended encirclement stacks with supply deprivation rapidly degrading combat power.

Encirclement vs. Direct Attack
It is almost always better to encircle a Polish formation than assault it frontally. A river-bank division at full strength may hold 3–4 turns under direct assault at considerable German cost. The same division, encircled and out of supply for two turns, often routs with a single attack.

Siege Combat

Warsaw and Lwów require siege reduction over multiple turns. The attacker must maintain the perimeter and provide artillery support each turn. Breaking the perimeter resets progress. Warsaw's garrison — boosted by popular support events — was among the most tenacious urban defences of the war.

§ 08 Air Power

Air power in Fall Weiss is heavily asymmetric — but the Polish Air Force in September 1939 was not destroyed on the ground on Day 1, as popular myth suggests. It had dispersed to secondary airfields and fought throughout the campaign, claiming around 300 German aircraft.

German Luftwaffe

  • Close Air Support: Attach to ground attacks for combat and morale bonuses. Stuka dive-bombers are particularly effective against isolated or encircled formations.
  • Interdiction: Target Polish railroad junctions, supply depots, and road bridges. Effective early interdiction can prevent Polish forces withdrawing to river lines in time.
Weather and Air Power
Bad weather turns ground Luftwaffe CAS, eliminating Germany's greatest tactical advantage for that turn. If weather deteriorates in the first week, consolidate rather than overextending Panzers without air cover.

Polish Air Force

Outclassed but not negligible. Can contest German CAS in specific battles, conduct ground attack against supply lines, and provide modest CAS to Polish attacks. Husband carefully — they cannot be replaced quickly and their loss leaves ground forces entirely exposed.

§ 09 Campaign Variants

The pre-turn variant system is one of Fall Weiss's most distinctive features. Both players select from a menu of historical and alternative options before Turn 1. These choices are binding and shape the entire game.

Historical Variant

Removes options and events that did not occur, including Polish early mobilisation (cancelled under Allied diplomatic pressure), significant Polish air modernisation, non-Soviet-invasion options, and Romanian intervention. Provides a game experience closely following the September Campaign's known progression. Meaningful uncertainty remains through variable reinforcement timing, card draws, and weather.

Alternative Variant Options

  • Polish early mobilisation: Full mobilisation before the German attack; improves initial Polish deployment. Historically cancelled to avoid provoking Germany.
  • Improved Polish air force: Additional modern fighters representing contracted aircraft that were historically delayed.
  • No Soviet invasion: The USSR stays neutral. Poland faces Germany on a single front — dramatically strengthens the Polish position.
  • Romanian intervention: Romania may provide military support, complicating German southern operations.
  • German complications: Cards representing diplomatic crises or military setbacks.
  • Pervitin effects: Enhanced methamphetamine use providing early movement bonuses at long-term morale cost.
Variant Recommendation for New Players
Start with the historical variant. It is more structured and allows learning base mechanics without extra variables. Once you've played 2–3 historical games, the alternative variant adds substantial replayability.

The Western Front Pressure Mechanic

Each turn Poland continues to resist, Allied pressure in the West increases slightly. If the track reaches its critical threshold — representing an Allied offensive while German forces are heavily committed in Poland — automatic German defeat fires. In the historical variant, the threshold is set very high (the Allies never came). In the alternative variant, with long Polish resistance and Allied Pressure cards, it can realistically be reached, making the Polish campaign a genuine strategic risk for Germany.

§ 10 Leaders

Leaders follow the standard SGS model — attaching to stacks to provide combat, morale, and movement bonuses. Fall Weiss leaders are particularly important for Germany, where individual commander quality was a genuine force multiplier.

Key German Leaders

Generaloberst Fedor von Bock
Army Group North Commander
Commands the northern axis from Pomerania and East Prussia. Solid all-round bonuses. Best used leading the drive to cut the Polish Corridor and subsequently pushing toward Warsaw from the north.
Generaloberst Gerd von Rundstedt
Army Group South Commander
The strongest overall German leader. High ATK, excellent morale multiplier, breakthrough capable. Best used with Panzer spearheads driving for the Vistula encirclement. Move him with the leading armoured element at all times.
General Heinz Guderian
XIX Motorised Corps
The armour theorist in his element. Exceptional ATK and breakthrough bonuses; able to chain breakthrough moves that shift entire Polish formations from viable defence to encirclement in a single turn. Never use him on infantry operations.
General Erich von Manstein
Chief of Staff, Army Group South
Primarily a card-synergy leader. Certain OKW Directive cards have enhanced effects when Manstein is active. Less direct as a tactical commander, valuable for unlocking strategic card combinations.

Key Polish Leaders

Marshal Edward Rýdz-Śmigły

Supreme commander. Provides coordination bonus and accesses high-level strategic cards. Moderate bonuses across all factors. Best near Warsaw where his presence stiffens the garrison and unlocks capital-defence events.
General Tadeusz Kutrzeba

The most aggressive Polish leader. High ATK; excellent morale multiplier. Architect of the Bzura counteroffensive. Use him for counterattacks — he is wasted in static defence.

§ 11 Cards & Events

The card system is richly thematic and provides high replayability through historical and hypothetical events covering diplomatic, military, political, and economic developments. No two campaigns play identically.

Card Categories

  • OKW Directives (German): High-level operational orders modifying German capabilities for one or more turns. Includes accelerated Panzer movement, priority air support, and logistics improvements.
  • Pervitin (German): Documented methamphetamine use by Wehrmacht troops. Provides early movement and combat bonuses but risks morale degradation if overused.
  • Polish Popular Support: Fired by Polish resistance; increases replacement rates and morale for garrison units. Strongest when accumulated and played during active siege turns.
  • Allied Pressure (Polish): Each card contributes to the Western Front Pressure track. The primary strategic tool for the Polish player in the alternative variant.
  • Diplomatic Events: Cover international reaction — British and French declarations of war, Soviet entry. Many fire automatically; some require activation.
  • Weather Cards: Both sides hold a small number that modify weather outcomes for one turn.
  • Counterintelligence / Sabotage: Can slow German reinforcement timing or disrupt Polish mobilisation.

German Card Priorities

  • Play OKW movement directives in Turns 1–3 to maximise encirclement reach before Polish forces reach river lines.
  • Hold Stuka cards for Warsaw and Lwów siege operations rather than spending them in open-field attacks Panzers can win alone.
  • Pervitin cards are strongest in Turns 1–4. Play them early.

Polish Card Priorities

  • Allied Pressure cards are your most strategic asset. Accumulate rather than spending early; the track compounds and the threat of auto-defeat is your leverage.
  • Popular Support cards save for Warsaw during siege operations.
  • Counterintelligence cards played early can delay specific German reinforcements and slow the encirclement timeline.

§ 12 The Soviet Invasion

On Turn 9 (representing 17 September 1939), the Soviet Union invades Poland from the east under the Molotov-Ribbentrop protocols. This event is one of the game's most dramatic turning points and forces the central Polish strategic dilemma.

The Eastern Deployment Dilemma

From Turn 1, the Polish player faces an agonising question: how many forces to keep facing east? Every unit kept on the eastern border is a unit not slowing the German advance. Every unit transferred west leaves the eastern flank open.

Eastern Border Decision Framework
Historical approach: Keep KOP in place; accept that eastern forces will be overwhelmed on Turn 9 regardless. This frees everything for the German front. KOP can delay the Soviets 2–3 turns on the Pripyat Marshes and Bug River.

Reinforced eastern deployment: Keep 3–4 regular divisions facing east. These can hold the Soviet advance 4–5 turns, viable only if the German front has stabilised or the alternative “no Soviet invasion” option has not been taken.

Soviet Operational Character

The post-purge Red Army of 1939 is not the force of 1944. The game models post-purge command paralysis through: lower ATK values than German equivalents despite similar unit sizes; reduced breakthrough capacity; morale fragility; and command radius limitations reducing operational flexibility.

Alternative Variant: No Soviet Invasion

If this option is selected in the pre-turn, the USSR stays neutral throughout. Poland concentrates everything against Germany. The Western Front Pressure mechanic becomes the primary German concern, and the campaign typically extends significantly beyond its historical length.

§ 13 Victory Conditions

Fall Weiss uses VP accumulation with special capitulation conditions that can end the game before the last turn. Understanding the difference between grinding VP and achieving capitulation is essential for both players.

Primary Conditions

  • Polish Capitulation: Poland surrenders when both Warsaw and Lwów are simultaneously held by Axis forces. This is the German primary objective. Not time-limited — Poland can theoretically hold until Turn 19.
  • Western Front Auto-Defeat (German): If the Western Front Pressure track reaches its critical threshold (driven by long Polish resistance and Allied Pressure cards), automatic German defeat fires regardless of the tactical situation.

VP Accumulation

  • Axis VPs accumulate each turn from controlled Polish cities. Warsaw and Lwów award the highest values; secondary cities award smaller amounts.
  • Polish VPs accumulate for each turn both cities remain in Polish hands, plus awards for destroying high-value German formations (especially Panzer divisions).

The Time Pressure

Germany cannot wait for Poland to collapse slowly. Each turn of delay builds Western Front Pressure and risks auto-defeat. This compels the German player to take operational risks — pushing Panzers beyond safe supply, committing reserves prematurely, assaulting fortified cities before siege forces are ready — mirroring the genuine tempo pressure the real Wehrmacht faced.

Axis Warning — VP Complacency
Many new German players focus on VP accumulation and neglect the Western Front Pressure track. If Poland plays Allied Pressure cards consistently and Warsaw holds past Turn 14, the auto-defeat trigger becomes a real threat in the alternative variant. Check the track every turn.

§ 15 Axis Strategy

The German objective is deceptively simple: take Warsaw and Lwów, force capitulation, and do it fast enough to avoid triggering Allied intervention. The art of the German game is achieving decisive encirclements — not just territorial advances — before Polish forces can establish coherent river-line defences.

Phase 1: The Opening Encirclements — Turns 1–4 (September 1–8)

Objectives: Secure the Polish Corridor; punch through Army Group South's border; begin the double envelopment trapping Polish forces west of the Vistula.

Form Kampfgruppen before advancing. Every Panzer division moves with motorised infantry and artillery support. Guderian's XIX Corps pushes hard for the Bug River, threatening Warsaw from the northeast. Rundstedt's Army Group South drives northeast toward Warsaw from Kraków. Do not stop to reduce isolated Polish formations — leave that to the standard infantry following behind. Play OKW movement directive cards in Turns 1–3.

Phase 2: Reducing the Cauldrons — Turns 5–8 (September 9–16)

Objectives: Close the Warsaw encirclement ring; reduce isolated armies in western cauldrons; isolate Lwów before Soviet entry complicates the picture.

Assign infantry divisions to systematically reduce encircled Polish pockets while motorised and Panzer forces drive east. Watch for Kutrzeba's Army of Poznań building on your northern flank — keep 2–3 infantry divisions screening against the Bzura counterattack threat. Begin Warsaw siege by Turn 6 with artillery and Stuka cards.

Phase 3: The Final Collapse — Turns 9–14 (September 17 – October 1)

Objectives: Coordinate with Soviet forces (historical); capture Lwów; force Warsaw's surrender; achieve capitulation before Western Front Pressure becomes critical.

Soviet entry dramatically simplifies your position. Polish forces facing east must now deal with a second threat; units withdrawn from your front no longer reinforce Warsaw. Focus remaining Panzer strength on Lwów while maintaining the Warsaw siege ring. Check the Western Front Pressure track every turn in the alternative variant.

Common German Mistakes

  • Incomplete encirclements: A Polish division that slips through a gap reconstitutes on the Vistula. Close every ring completely.
  • Wasting Panzers on sieges: Panzers take disproportionate losses in city assaults. Use them to encircle; leave reduction to infantry and artillery.
  • Ignoring Western Front Pressure: In the alternative variant, this is a losing condition. Check it every turn and manage through speed.
  • Supply overextension: Check supply lines before every Panzer advance, not after they've already moved and found themselves isolated.

§ 16 Polish Strategy

Playing Poland in SGS Fall Weiss is genuinely difficult and genuinely rewarding. Victory for Poland is measured in time bought, German losses inflicted, and the Western Front Pressure track value when the game ends. You cannot defeat Germany; you can make the campaign costly, slow, and pyrrhic.

Phase 1: Delaying the Encirclement — Turns 1–4 (September 1–8)

Objectives: Get best infantry divisions onto major river lines (Vistula, San, Narew) before German Panzers cut roads behind them; accept that border positions will be lost; preserve the Warsaw garrison at full strength.

Do not fight on the open plain. Border infantry conducts fighting withdrawals toward river lines; cavalry screens flanks, delays motorised spearheads in forests, and raids supply lines. Anders harassing German supply lines is more valuable than him defending a river crossing. Sacrifice isolated or encircled units rather than attempting open-plain breakouts.

Phase 2: The Defensive Core — Turns 5–8 (September 9–16)

Objectives: Hold Warsaw and Lwów; execute the Bzura counterattack if conditions allow; accumulate Allied Pressure cards; decide eastern deployment before Soviet entry.

The Bzura window opens Turns 5–6. If Kutrzeba's Army of Poznań is intact on the German northern flank, launching the counterattack is nearly always correct. Even without breakthrough, it disrupts German operations 2–3 turns, slows the Warsaw investment, and costs irreplaceable motorised formations. Accumulate Allied Pressure cards seriously from Turn 5. Decide your eastern stance by Turn 7–8.

Phase 3: The Siege — Turns 9–19 (September 17 – October 6)

Objectives: Keep Warsaw and Lwów fighting; play Popular Support cards to sustain garrisons; maximise Western Front Pressure accumulation; make every German siege turn costly.

Play Popular Support cards for Warsaw every siege turn. Rómmel in Warsaw provides significant DEF; keep him there permanently from Turn 5. The longer Warsaw holds, the more Allied Pressure cards you can accumulate; in the alternative variant, every extra turn of resistance potentially moves the auto-defeat trigger closer to fruition.

Common Polish Mistakes

  • Fighting on the open plain: Every Polish unit lost in open terrain is a unit that never made it to the Vistula. Withdraw to defensible ground; accept temporary German advances.
  • Neglecting the Bzura opportunity: Kutrzeba's window is one of the few moments where Polish forces can inflict genuine strategic pain. Do not waste the Army of Poznań in static defence when the counterattack window is open.
  • Dispersing the Warsaw garrison: Every unit pulled from Warsaw for a gap elsewhere reduces siege resistance. Rómmel and the full garrison should stay in Warsaw from Turn 5 onward.
  • Ignoring the eastern deployment decision: Failing to decide your eastern stance leaves you with the worst of both worlds. Commit early.

§ 17 PBEM & Multiplayer

SGS Fall Weiss supports Play By Email (PBEM) for two or three human players. The three-player variant (Germany, Poland, Soviet Union) adds a distinctive diplomatic layer as the Soviet player's timing and aggression can vary significantly from the historical outcome.

How PBEM Works

  1. Player 1 (Axis) takes their turn, saves the file, sends it to Player 2.
  2. Player 2 (Poland) loads, takes their turn, returns the file.
  3. In the three-player variant, the Soviet player takes their turn from Turn 9 onward.
  4. Continues until Poland capitulates or a victory condition triggers.

Full PBEM instructions: strategygamestudio.com/sgs-rules/multiplayer

Three-Player PBEM Dynamics

The Soviet player has genuine agency over when and how aggressively the USSR invades. A conservative Soviet player gives Poland more time to concentrate against Germany. An aggressive Soviet player creates a genuine two-front crisis. Germany cannot directly influence Soviet decisions — adding uncertainty that is historically authentic.

PBEM Strategy Notes

  • Write strategic notes between sessions — a 19-turn game played over 2–3 weeks requires remembering your entire framework.
  • Agree whether pre-turn option selections are revealed or kept hidden before starting.
  • Weather card use is particularly powerful in PBEM because your opponent cannot see your hand. Polish storm cards played in a German turn where Stukas were planned can reverse a battle entirely.
  • In the alternative variant, the Western Front Pressure track requires both players to track carefully across sessions. Keep a running note of its current value.

§ 18 Glossary

Alternative Variant
Pre-turn option unlocking historical hypotheticals: early mobilisation, improved Polish air, no Soviet invasion, Romanian intervention.
Allied Pressure
Polish card type; each builds the Western Front Pressure track. Primary strategic tool for Poland in the alternative variant.
Army Group North
German northern force under Bock; attacks from Pomerania and East Prussia. Responsible for the Polish Corridor and northern envelopment.
Army Group South
German main armoured force under Rundstedt; drives from Silesia and Slovakia toward Warsaw and the Vistula encirclement.
ATK
Attack factor; governs offensive combat die rolls.
Battle Morale
Sum of unit morale + leader + terrain + card modifiers; routing occurs when it reaches zero. Encircled units suffer Battle Morale penalties.
Breakthrough
Post-victory advance by eligible units (armour, motorised, leaders). Kampfgruppe formations extend breakthrough range.
Bzura
River and battle; the largest Polish counteroffensive (9–12 September), launched by the Army of Poznań against the German flank. A scenario in its own right.
Capitulation
Polish defeat condition; triggered when both Warsaw and Lwów are simultaneously held by Axis forces.
DEF
Defense factor; governs defensive combat rolls. Modified by terrain type and fortification.
Encirclement
All adjacent regions held by enemy; encircled units suffer combat and morale penalties and cannot receive reinforcements.
FoW
Fog of War; enemy units hidden unless adjacent or within reconnaissance range.
Historical Variant
Pre-turn option restricting choices to those that historically occurred; more scripted, closer to the actual September Campaign.
Kampfgruppe
Combined-arms German formation (Panzer + motorised + artillery); receives ATK, movement, and breakthrough bonuses. Core German offensive doctrine.
KOP
Korpus Ochrony Pogranicza; Polish eastern frontier troops. Central piece of the eastern deployment dilemma.
Life Points
Unit strength; at 0 LP unit is eliminated. Replacements restore LP to maximum for supplied units.
Lwów
Fortress city in southeastern Poland; with Warsaw, required for Axis capitulation condition. Faces German and Soviet pressure from Turn 9.
Molotov-Ribbentrop
Nazi-Soviet pact (23 August 1939); secret protocol partitions Poland. Triggers automatic Soviet entry event on Turn 9 in historical variant.
MOV / MP
Movement points; spent crossing region connections per phase. Panzer and motorised units have highest MOV values.
MOR
Morale factor; contributes to Battle Morale. Polish cavalry and regular infantry have high MOR; reserve divisions lower.
PBEM
Play By Email; asynchronous 2- or 3-player multiplayer mode.
Pervitin
German card; documents methamphetamine use by Wehrmacht troops. Provides early movement and combat bonuses; historically attested.
Popular Support
Polish card type; boosts Warsaw garrison morale and replacement rates. Strongest during active siege turns.
Pre-Turn
Campaign setup phase before Turn 1; both players select historical or alternative options. Choices are binding for the entire game.
Pursuit
Free fire against routed units after a rout result; costly for already-weakened formations.
Relay (Supply)
Friendly town, depot, or logistics unit extending supply up to five additional regions.
Rout
Catastrophic combat failure; battle ends, routing units take Pursuit fire.
SGS
Strategy Game Studio; game engine and developer name.
Supply Range
Five regions from a source or relay. Unsupplied units cannot receive replacements and suffer combat penalties.
VP
Victory Points; accumulated per turn from controlled objectives. Determines margin of victory when capitulation is the primary condition.
Warsaw
Polish capital; fortified city; required Axis capture for capitulation. Defended by Popular Support events and Rómmel's leadership bonus.
Western Front Pressure
Track accumulating from Polish resistance and Allied Pressure cards. Critical threshold triggers automatic German defeat in the alternative variant.
