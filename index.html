§ 01 Historical Context

SGS Taipings simulates one of history's most devastating conflicts: the Taiping Rebellion (1851–1864), a cataclysmic civil war in mid-19th-century China that claimed an estimated 20–30 million lives — one of the deadliest wars in human history.

Origins of the Rebellion

The rebellion's roots lay in the charisma of Hong Xiuquan, a Hakka Chinese man from Guangdong Province who, after repeatedly failing the imperial examinations, experienced religious visions in which he believed himself to be the younger brother of Jesus Christ. He founded the "Bai Shangdi Hui" (God Worshippers Society) and began preaching a syncretic Christian-influenced doctrine that drew thousands of impoverished peasants, displaced miners, and disaffected Hakka and Zhuang peoples.

The Qing dynasty in 1851 was already battered: economically ruined by the Opium Wars (1839–42, 1856–60), facing rampant corruption, severe famine, and a flood crisis along the Yellow River. The Manchu ruling class was seen as illegitimate foreign overlords by many Han Chinese, and their military — the Banner Armies and Green Standard troops — had stagnated into hollow, underfunded shells.

The Heavenly Kingdom

In January 1851, Hong Xiuquan proclaimed the Taiping Tianguo (Heavenly Kingdom of Great Peace) with himself as Heavenly King. The movement rapidly swept north through Guangxi and Hunan, capturing Nanjing in March 1853 and renaming it Tianjing (Heavenly Capital). At its height the Taiping state controlled large swaths of the Yangtze Valley — the richest agricultural and commercial heartland of China.

The Taiping introduced radical social reforms: gender equality in theory (separate women's battalions), land redistribution, prohibition of opium, foot-binding, and prostitution. However, the theocratic leadership grew increasingly autocratic and fractious, culminating in the catastrophic Tianjing Massacre of 1856 when the Taiping kings turned on each other, killing tens of thousands of followers.

Foreign Involvement

Britain and France, fresh from the Second Opium War, initially maintained cautious neutrality. By 1860–61, attitudes shifted. The Ever Victorious Army (EVA) — Chinese troops trained and led by Western officers including Frederick Townsend Ward and later Charles "Chinese" Gordon — fought for the Qing, introducing modern tactics and weaponry that accelerated the dynasty's recovery.

Other Rebellions

The game also models several concurrent rebellions that complicated Qing strategic planning:

  • The Nien Rebellion (1851–1868) — a bandit confederation operating in northern China along the Huang-Ho River plain, tying down substantial Qing forces.
  • The Panthay Rebellion in Yunnan — Muslim uprising (1856–1873).
  • The Miao Rebellion in Guizhou and Hunan.
Why this matters for gameplay

The historical arc drives the game's asymmetry. The Taipings begin with momentum and the element of surprise. The Qing begin weak, slow, and hamstrung by bureaucratic inertia. Both sides race against time in opposite directions.

The Two Sides

The Taipings (Heavenly Kingdom) Taiping

You command Hong Xiuquan's rebel state: Taiping armies and navies, allied Nien bandits, and event-triggered rebel groups. Your early game is aggressive — you have momentum, initiative, and quality units that outclass the initial Qing forces. Your long-term challenge is internal cohesion and the growing Qing response once the Imperial Concern index rises.

  • Strengths: High initial unit quality, offensive initiative, surprise, multiple allied factions.
  • Weaknesses: Internal leadership feuds (event-triggered), limited starting territory, must hold economically vital Yangtze cities.

The Qing Dynasty Qing

You command the Manchu imperial apparatus: Banner Armies, Green Standard troops, provincial militias, and eventually the modern Xiang Army and Ever Victorious Army. Your early game is reactive and painful — you are slow, poorly led, and spread thin. The Qing game is a race to raise your Imperial Concern, unlock better units and leaders, and stabilise the front.

  • Strengths: Vast territory, long-term economic advantage, access to foreign troops, more strategic depth.
  • Weaknesses: Poor initial unit quality, bad leadership, bureaucratic delays via the Imperial Concern gate, reactive to rebel initiative.

The Five Core Gameplay Pillars

  1. Area-based movement and combat on an irregular map of Chinese provinces and regions.
  2. An economic engine: income from cities, ports, and agricultural regions funds unit construction and maintenance.
  3. A supply system that degrades unsupplied units progressively until eliminated.
  4. The Loyalty system (v1.5): regions must be actively garrisoned or they revert to the original owner.
  5. A card and event system providing diplomatic, military, and economic surprises each turn.

§ 03 Turn Structure & Phase Guide

Every game turn represents one calendar month. Phases resolve in strict sequence. Understanding the order is critical because card timings, retreats, and reinforcements all depend on phase placement.

01
Cards Phase
Each side receives cards for the turn. Events may fire automatically. Play strategic cards with no phase restriction now; save combat cards for later.
02
Reinforcements Phase
New units arrive at designated entry points per scenario rules. New units cannot move this turn but can defend.
03
Supply Phase
Supply is traced for all stacks. Unsupplied units begin taking movement and combat penalties. Identify cut-off stacks; play logistics cards if available.
04
Naval Movement Phase
Naval units move along coast and rivers. Use rivers to outflank; protect supply lines; land/embark troops at ports.
05
Naval Combat Phase
Naval battles resolve. Engage enemy fleets to contest river and coastal supply.
06
Land Movement Phase
Land units move, attacking or repositioning. Declare attacks by entering enemy regions. Concentric attacks from multiple regions grant combat bonuses.
07
Land Combat Phase
All battles resolve simultaneously by region. Play combat cards during battles. Use leaders to boost morale at critical moments.
08
Replacement Phase
Supplied units in range of replacements may be rebuilt to full strength. Prioritise elite or strategically positioned units.
09
Victory Check Phase
VP totals calculated. Sudden death conditions checked. Review score and adjust strategy accordingly.
10
End Phase
Turn counter advances. Save game if PBEM; review the combat log for the next planning cycle.
Critical Timing Note

Cards marked "play during movement" must be played in Phase 6 before the relevant movement occurs. Cards marked "play during combat" are used in Phase 7 as a battle unfolds. Always read each card's timing restriction before holding it in hand.

The Yangtze River Corridor

The Yangtze is the spine of the game. It flows through the richest agricultural and commercial regions. Rich income sources and entry points for foreign influence. British and French powers may intervene here. Control of Shanghai is extremely valuable economically and diplomatically.

Beijing

The Qing imperial capital. Far north and initially safe, but vulnerable if the Qing front collapses. Some scenarios include a Taiping northward-drive strategy targeting Beijing.

§ 06 Supply, Movement & Stacking

Supply

Supply is the lifeblood of your armies. An unsupplied stack suffers progressively: first reduced movement, then reduced combat values, then life-point loss each turn until eliminated. Cutting enemy supply is often more effective than frontal assault.

  • A stack is in supply if it can trace an unbroken path, free of enemy units, back to a supply source or relay.
  • Supply sources are indicated on the map (major cities or specially marked regions).
  • Supply relays are friendly structures (cities, ports, forts) and certain logistics units that extend the chain.
  • Supply has a maximum range of 3–4 regions in most scenarios.
  • Certain terrain (impassable mountains) blocks supply entirely.
Tactical Tip — The Yangtze Highway

The Yangtze River doubles as a supply highway. Naval units moving on the Yangtze can act as floating supply relays, extending your supply reach deep into enemy territory. Always maintain a riverine presence; losing the river often means losing supply to your forward armies.

Movement

Stacks spend movement points (MP) to move from region to region. Each connection's cost is shown in tooltips. A stack may pass through a friendly region even if almost fully stacked (so long as it does not stop there).

  1. Land units move in the Land Movement Phase only (Phase 6).
  2. Naval units move in the Naval Movement Phase (Phase 4).
  3. Entering an enemy-occupied region declares an attack; battle resolves in Phase 7.
  4. Multiple stacks from different regions can attack the same target — this is a concentric attack and gives a combat bonus.
  5. Immobilized units cannot move; right-click move will auto-select only mobile units in mixed stacks (v1.5 improvement).

§ 07 Combat System In Depth

Combat in SGS Taipings is probabilistic but not random — stacking quality units, attaching good leaders, attacking from multiple directions, and playing the right combat cards all shift the odds substantially.

Battle Resolution Sequence

  1. Attacker enters defender's region during Land Movement Phase.
  2. Multiple attacking stacks from different directions merge into one combined force.
  3. Battle opens with preliminary rounds: artillery, reconnaissance, ambushes fire first.
  4. Main round: all surviving land units fight simultaneously.
  5. After the main round, either side may retreat. The non-retreating side wins.
  6. Post-main rounds continue with escalating battle exhaustion (morale drops each round).
  7. If a side's Battle Morale reaches 0 and fails a rout test, the battle ends in rout. A pursuit round may fire on fleeing units.

The Die Roll Mechanic

For each unit in a round, one die is rolled. If the result is equal to or less than the unit's modified combat factor, it inflicts one hit on the opposing side. A result of 0 always hits regardless of modifiers. Hits are distributed across the enemy side proportionally to unit strengths.

Battle Morale

Battle Morale = (mean morale of combat units) + (leader's morale value) + terrain effects + booster bonuses (cards or special units). Morale is capped at 9 maximum. Combat bonuses from all sources combined are capped at −4/+4.

Critical Cap Rule

Morale boosters are hard-capped at +4 in SGS Taipings specifically. Stacking three great leaders won't give you +9 — you are capped at +4 total boost. Plan your leader deployments accordingly and don't waste multiple elite leaders in a single stack that's already at the cap.

Retreats & Breakthroughs

After the main round, either side may retreat. Attackers return to the region they entered from; defenders retreat to an adjacent friendly region. Units jump over fully-stacked friendly regions when retreating. A key v1.5 rule: retreat into a region loyal to you is possible even if the enemy just captured it.

Breakthrough: if the attacker wins and has breakthrough-capable units (cavalry, fast-moving leaders), they may immediately advance into an adjacent region — potentially starting another battle. Chains of breakthroughs with expert leaders can create dramatic encirclements.

§ 08 Sieges & Fortresses

Fortresses are structures in certain regions displaying a Defense Factor and capable of containing garrisoned units. They cannot be captured by passing through — they must be besieged or assaulted.

Siege Resolution

  • Siege begins when at least 2 of your units are outside the fortress in the same region.
  • Each turn, a die is rolled. On a modified result ≤ the fortress's Defense Factor, the fortress surrenders and its garrison is eliminated.
  • Multiple besieging units reduce the effective Defense Factor — mass your forces for faster resolution.

Assault

  • Assault is a direct attack on the fortress rather than waiting for surrender.
  • Treated as a normal battle, but the fortress garrison and structure provide a massive defensive bonus (+2 to +4).
  • Assault is faster than siege but costly. Only attempt with substantial numerical and morale superiority, or after prolonged siege has weakened the garrison.
Pro Tip — Combined Siege/Assault

Begin a siege to whittle down the Defense Factor over several turns, then switch to assault once the fortress is weakened. Always bring artillery to sieges — it fires in the preliminary round and can devastate fortress defenders before the main combat even begins.

§ 09 Economy & Income

The economic system in SGS Taipings is a unique addition to the SGS engine. Income funds unit construction, maintenance, and certain card/event effects. Mismanaging your economy starves your army.

Income Sources

  • Major Cities & Ports: the primary income generators. Yangtze cities and coastal ports produce significantly more than interior towns.
  • Agricultural Regions (Rice Fields): rice-growing regions produce income; controlling the Yangtze valley rice bowl is economically transformative.
  • Trade Routes: control of coastal and river trade routes provides bonus income.
  • Foreign Trade: certain diplomacy events and card plays unlock foreign income streams.

Income Modifiers

  • Loyalty: a region at 50% loyalty to you yields only 50% of its income. Full loyalty = full income.
  • Corruption & Imperial Spending (Qing only): a corruption mechanic wastes a portion of income; rises as Imperial Concern rises and modernisation occurs.
  • Nien cavalry raiding: can significantly cut income without ever capturing a city — raids on agricultural regions are highly cost-effective for the Taipings.
Economic Priority — Taiping

Seize the Yangtze cities as fast as possible. Nanjing, Wuhan, Anqing, and Zhenjiang together generate enough income to sustain a major army. Do not overextend so far that you cannot hold and loyalise these core cities — a disloyal Yangtze city produces zero income for you.

Economic Priority — Qing

Protect your coastal ports (Shanghai especially) from Taiping raids. Deny the rebels the Yangtze income by contesting river cities. Use income to accelerate Imperial Concern spending and unlock better forces as quickly as possible.

§ 10 The Loyalty System

Added in the 2024 v1.5 major update, the Loyalty system fundamentally changed how territorial control works. It is arguably the single most important mechanic to understand in the modern version of the game.

How Loyalty Works

  • Every region has a loyalty value ranging from 0% to 100% for each side.
  • Capturing a region does not instantly convert it. Loyalty shifts toward you only if you maintain military presence there.
  • If you leave a captured region ungarrisoned, its loyalty gradually returns to the original owner — and may flip back.
  • Disloyal regions allow your opponent to construct new units there, even behind your front lines.

Loyalty Strategy

Taiping Loyalty Strategy

  • Never advance so fast that you leave a trail of disloyal regions behind you. Enemy militia can suddenly appear in "captured" territory you left empty.
  • Leave small garrison units to hold key corridors. Even a single militia unit accelerates loyalty conversion.
  • Prioritise loyalty in income-generating regions first.

Qing Loyalty Strategy

  • Loyalty is your greatest long-term weapon. Your empire starts with high loyalty in most of China — use that advantage.
  • When you lose territory, the loyalty bleed is your chance to regenerate. Move forces through recaptured areas and let loyalty restore income.
  • Use Unrest units (added in v1.5) to degrade loyalty in Taiping-held regions, cutting their income without direct combat.
Key Insight

A deep Taiping breakthrough that overruns empty Qing territory may leave a string of regions that never truly become Taiping-loyal, creating a fragile, indefensible salient. "Holding the line" means maintaining garrisoned presence in every region you claim — not just a front line.

The Ever Victorious Army

If the Qing successfully courts British support, the Ever Victorious Army (EVA) becomes available. Commanded first by Frederick Townsend Ward and later by Charles Gordon, it is an elite mixed force with high attack, defense, and morale. The EVA operates primarily in Jiangsu Province near Shanghai. Ward's death in 1862 is a scripted event; Gordon then takes command.

Diplomacy Warning — Both Sides

Do not assume the foreign powers will stay on your side. Historical events can flip diplomatic allegiances. The British were pragmatic — they cared about trade, not ideology. Actions that threaten Shanghai or disrupt commerce can suddenly turn a friendly power hostile. Monitor the diplomatic track every turn.

§ 13 The Imperial Concern Index

The Imperial Concern Index is the Qing-exclusive mechanic that models the Manchu court's slow awakening to the severity of the rebellion. It is the central challenge and drama of playing the Qing side.

What It Restricts at Start

  • Units: only weak, cheap Green Standard and Banner units available initially.
  • Leaders: poor-quality generals only; Zeng Guofan and Li Hongzhang are locked behind IC thresholds.
  • Spending: limited ability to fund modernization or foreign-weapon purchases.
  • Corruption: high corruption modifier bleeds your income until IC rises.

IC Thresholds & Unlocks

Medium (26–50)Court alarmed; governors mobilisingZeng Guofan commission eligible. Xiang Army construction begins. Reduced corruption.
High (51–75)Full imperial mobilisationLi Hongzhang available. Foreign weapon purchases allowed. EVA option if diplomacy supports. Modern artillery.
Maximum (76–100)Crisis recognised as existentialAll top leaders available. Xiang Army at full strength. Minimal corruption. Full modernisation unlocked.
Core Qing Insight — The Controlled Crisis

As Qing, you almost want the Taipings to capture major cities early — because it raises your IC faster. The problem is that letting them take too much territory means you may never financially recover. The ideal Qing strategy is a "controlled crisis": let the Taipings advance just enough to spike your IC, while delaying their capture of the most critical economic cities.

§ 15 Playing the Taipings

The Taipings are the aggressor. You start with initiative, quality, and surprise. Your window to win is the first 30–50 turns before the Qing fully mobilises. Everything in your strategy must serve one master goal: establish and consolidate the Heavenly Kingdom's economic base before Imperial Concern unlocks the Qing's true potential.

Phase 1: The Great March North (Turns 1–15)

Objectives: capture Nanjing as fast as possible, secure the Yangtze corridor from Wuhan to Zhenjiang, neutralise or bypass weak Qing garrisons.

Your Taiping infantry outclasses the initial Green Standard Qing troops. Do not give the Qing time to entrench. Use cavalry and fast infantry to race ahead and cut supply lines — isolated Green Standard units will wither without a fight. Avoid expensive frontal assaults on fortified cities unless you have artillery support. Bring Taiping artillery forward as fast as movement allows.

The Yangtze is your highway. Establish naval control immediately. In early turns your river fleet can provide supply relay, transport troops, and block Qing river movement. Prioritise the river above almost all other concerns.

Phase 2: Consolidation (Turns 15–40)

Objectives: loyalise all captured cities, build income-producing infrastructure, construct reserve armies, manage the Northern Expedition carefully.

This is the most dangerous phase. You have a lot of territory but loyalty conversion lags behind your front lines. The Qing will probe your rear with cavalry raids and militia materialising in disloyal regions you thought you controlled. Establish a garrison network: every captured city should have at least 1–2 units permanently stationed.

The Northern Expedition (historical push toward Beijing) is tempting but strategically disastrous if overcommitted. Treat it as a pinning operation to raise Qing Imperial Concern, not a war-winning stroke.

The Tianjing Massacre Event (~Turn 60–65)

This event fires around the historical date of 1856 and removes key Taiping leaders, imposing significant penalties. In v1.5 you have alternative choices. Recommendation: if you have the choice, spare Yang Xiuqing or pay a price — losing multiple high-morale leaders simultaneously is game-changing. Build up your position before this event fires; you need to be strong enough to absorb the shock.

Phase 3: Endgame (Turns 50+)

If you are winning: push hard. Advance on flanks, siege secondary Qing cities, use your income advantage to build additional elite units. Deny the Qing player time.

If the Qing are recovering: once Xiang Army units appear with Zeng Guofan, switch to defensive play. Hold your economic core, fortify Nanjing deeply, and make every Qing attack costly. Bleed them — their corruption mechanic and bureaucratic inefficiencies create long-term strain. Let events work for you.

Taiping Build Order Priority

  1. Taiping Elite Infantry — your core combat power.
  2. Taiping Artillery — essential for sieges; never neglect.
  3. Taiping Cavalry — supply-cutting and exploitation.
  4. Garrison Militia — cheap loyalty-conversion units; critical for the Loyalty system.
  5. Taiping Warships — only if the Yangtze is actively contested.

Key Taiping Card Plays

  • Play diplomatic cards to keep France and Britain neutral. The EVA is your greatest long-term threat.
  • Use reconnaissance cards aggressively early — knowing where thin Qing lines are lets you exploit gaps.
  • Hold 1–2 powerful combat cards in reserve for the inevitable Qing counter-offensive. They will come with a big stack; you want a battle card to blunt it.

§ 16 Playing the Qing

The Qing is the designated "hard" side (per the developers). You start weak, slow, and outclassed. Your game is about survival, patience, and timing your resurgence perfectly. It is arguably the more intellectually demanding side to play well.

Most Common Beginner Qing Mistake

Trying to hold the entire front line with Green Standard troops. This spreads your weak forces thin, they get beaten in detail, and you run out of units. Instead, concentrate at key chokepoint nodes, let the Taipings advance into disloyal territory they cannot easily garrison, and prepare your counter once Imperial Concern rises.

Phase 1: Controlled Retreat (Turns 1–20)

Objectives: do not try to stop the Taipings with Green Standard troops in the open; trade space for time; keep supply lines open; spike Imperial Concern with IC-raising cards every single turn.

Green Standard infantry cannot stop Taiping elite troops in open battle. Concentrate your forces at chokepoints where terrain modifiers compensate for unit quality. Fortified city defenders gain +2 defense — even weak Qing troops become reasonably hard to dislodge in a fortress.

Protect the coastal ports. Shanghai and Guangzhou generate enormous income. If you lose them early, you may never financially recover. Position your best early units (Manchu Banner cavalry) to screen the coastline.

Phase 2: Imperial Awakening (Turns 15–40)

Objectives: raise Imperial Concern to at least the "Medium" threshold; begin Xiang Army construction; court British/French support; conduct naval operations to contest the Yangtze.

Once Zeng Guofan appears, your strategic situation transforms. The Xiang Army is roughly on par with Taiping elite infantry and superior in morale when stacked with Zeng Guofan. Form concentrated striking forces of 3–4 Xiang Army units under Zeng and begin methodical offensive operations along the Yangtze.

Use Qing cavalry to cut Taiping supply lines. You cannot out-fight them everywhere at once, but you can isolate forward Taiping stacks — an isolated elite Taiping stack starves within 3–4 turns.

Phase 3: The Counter-Offensive (Turns 40+)

Objectives: retake Yangtze cities one at a time from the east (where EVA support is available); methodically loyalise recaptured regions; besiege Nanjing with Zeng Guofan's full army.

The historical Qing strategy — optimal in-game — was the "ever-tightening ring." Recapture Anqing to cut the Yangtze link, then advance on Nanjing from multiple directions. Concentric attacks with 4 Xiang Army stacks converging from three directions on a Taiping position will consistently win.

Qing Build Order Priority

  1. Xiang Army Infantry — as many as you can afford; your game-winning tool.
  2. Xiang Army Cavalry — fast, powerful, excellent supply-cutters.
  3. Modern Artillery — essential for reducing Taiping fortresses; build as soon as unlocked.
  4. Qing Warships — Yangtze control; outclass Taiping junks significantly.
  5. Provincial Militia — cheap garrison fillers to hold recaptured regions and accelerate loyalty.

Managing the Nien Threat

The Nien Bandits in the north are an ongoing drain. They are highly mobile and excel at raiding agricultural regions, cutting income and supply.

  • Keep 1–2 Banner cavalry units dedicated to the northern theater to chase and destroy Nien forces.
  • Sengge Rinchen (Mongol cavalry leader) is the ideal Nien-fighter; position him in the north as soon as he's available.
  • Do not let Nien raiding paralyse your northern income, but do not over-commit resources away from the Taiping front.

§ 17 PBEM & Multiplayer

SGS Taipings supports Play By Email (PBEM) multiplayer for two human players. The PBEM system allows each player to take their turn asynchronously — ideal for wargamers with busy schedules.

How PBEM Works

  1. Player 1 (usually Taiping) takes their turn, then saves and sends the game file to Player 2.
  2. Player 2 (Qing) receives the file, loads it, takes their turn, and returns the file.
  3. This continues until the scenario ends.

Detailed PBEM setup instructions are available in Chinese and English at the official site: strategygamestudio.com/sgs-rules/multiplayer

PBEM Strategy Differences

  • Write notes about your strategic intentions between sessions — you cannot remember every plan across days.
  • Fog of War is more important in PBEM; dedicate more intelligence/reconnaissance card plays since you cannot watch what the opponent is doing.
  • Agree on a turn cadence with your opponent before starting a grand campaign (e.g., turns within 48 hours).

§ 18 Glossary

ATK
Attack factor; governs offensive combat die rolls.
Banner
Traditional Qing cavalry/infantry units; initially mediocre quality.
Battle Morale
Sum of unit morale + leader bonus + terrain + boosters; governs routing.
Breakthrough
Post-victory advance into adjacent region; can chain with expert leaders.
Concentric
Attacking from 2+ regions simultaneously; grants attacker a combat bonus.
DEF
Defense factor; governs defensive combat die rolls.
EVA
Ever Victorious Army; Western-led Qing allied elite force.
FoW
Fog of War; enemy units in low-loyalty regions are hidden.
Green Standard
Cheap but weak Qing infantry; the initial Qing backbone.
IC
Imperial Concern Index (Qing); rises as crisis proves serious; unlocks better forces.
Life Points
Unit strength; at 0 the unit is eliminated.
Loyalty
Regional affiliation (0–100%); affects income, FoW, and enemy unit construction.
MOV / MP
Movement points; spent crossing region connections per phase.
MOR
Morale factor; contributes to Battle Morale calculation.
Nien Bandits
Northern Chinese rebel cavalry alliance; Taiping allied faction.
PBEM
Play By Email; asynchronous 2-player multiplayer mode.
Pursuit
Free round of fire against routed units after a rout result.
Relay (Supply)
Friendly structure or logistics unit extending supply range.
Rout
Catastrophic combat failure; battle ends, routed units take pursuit fire.
SGS
Strategy Game Studio; game engine and developer name.
Supply Range
Maximum region distance from a source/relay; typically 3–4 regions.
Tianjing
Nanjing renamed as the Taiping Heavenly Capital.
Unrest Units
v1.5 units that degrade loyalty in enemy-held regions without direct combat.
VP
Victory Points; accumulated per scenario objectives.
Xiang Army
Zeng Guofan's reformed Qing army; unlocked by IC; core Qing endgame force.
Yangtze
The great river; economic spine of China and central strategic axis of the game.
